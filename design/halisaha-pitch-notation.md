# Pitch Notation

**A tactical-cartographic visual movement.** The pitch is the page. The line is the alphabet. The formation is the sentence. Communication occurs through coordinate, geometric weight, and the precise placement of marks across negative space — never through paragraphs, never through ornament. The viewer reads the work the way one reads a star chart, an early-modern map, or the chalkboard of a master coach: the meaning lives between the lines.

The visual expression is severe and contemplative. A single field — drawn at the proportions of a regulation pitch — anchors the composition with monumental presence. White-line markings are rendered as ink hairlines on a linen-toned ground, recalling the printed plates of a continental atlas more than the photograph of a stadium. Players appear as numbered dots, their positions held with the gravity of celestial bodies in an astronomical diagram. One — and only one — element bears a warm accent: a single amber mark, the residue of a setting sun on the artificial grass, the lit-up pitch at dusk, the referee's flag. Every other element submits to a single deep emerald ink, unified by the discipline of one master plate.

Typography is rare and architectural. The wordmark is monumental, set in a tall condensed display whose presence borrows from Polish poster school energy and Bauhaus structural confidence. Surrounding it, all other type is whispered: small monospace annotations behaving as map ticks, tiny coordinate labels that anchor the eye, an italic serif tagline placed once with the restraint of a museum caption. Words are not paragraphs — they are reference markers in a system whose primary speech is geometric. The overall feeling: a single page from an imaginary discipline of football cartography, the plate marked _FIG. I_, the volume marked in Roman numerals.

Composition operates on a tactical grid. Elements snap to a coordinate system that mimics the pitch itself: penalty spots, half-line, axis. The work breathes through generous margins of unmarked linen, with every mark earning its presence the way a seismic line earns its presence on graph paper. There is no decoration. No gradient. No shadow. There is only line, dot, and the weight of a single chosen color against negative space. The viewer should feel the composition is the inevitable result of a master craftsman who has labored for countless hours, removing everything that did not deserve to remain — that one displaced player would break the harmony entirely.

The aesthetic owes its discipline to systematic observation: Otl Aicher's Munich pictograms, the chalk diagrams of Rinus Michels, the meditative restraint of Japanese photobooks, the architectural rigor of Swiss cartography. It treats the casual, the everyday, the neighborhood ritual of pickup football, with the reverence usually reserved for celestial maps and engineering plates. The contrast is the art statement: an artifact that proves something ephemeral — friendship, weekend matches, the lit-up evening pitch — can be studied, mapped, and rendered with the patience of a discipline.

The result must read as the painstaking work of someone at the absolute top of their field. Every line weight calibrated, every alignment refined to the millimeter, every spatial relationship the product of countless quiet decisions. The artifact should feel inevitable — as though no other arrangement could have been correct, as though the page has existed in this exact configuration since the first chalk was dragged across the first board. A coffee-table-book plate. A museum specimen. A field marshal's diagram of a beautiful, ordinary game.
